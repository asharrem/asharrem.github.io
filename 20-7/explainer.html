From Rogernomics to a finished pivot

The 1986 "Rogernomics" reforms began New Zealand's shift from heavy income tax toward GST, but stopped halfway: we kept large PAYE and company bases. The 20/7 proposal is the argument for completing that journey—moving tax off creation (wages and accounting profit) and onto velocity (spending and supply-chain flow). In story terms, the Crown steps back from being a silent partner in your gross pay and becomes a visible clip on invoices and tills—controversial at the checkout, but meant to reward every extra hour at the margin because income tax at source goes to zero in the blueprint.

The handbrake and the engine

One way to read the contrast is psychological: brackets and provisional tax train people to think about tax before effort; GST trains them to think about it when money moves. The table is schematic, not a statutory description.

Feature The old way (productivity brake) The new way (velocity engine)
Primary target Taxing creation (your labour and profit). Taxing process (flow and consumption).
Incentive Higher brackets bite harder as you earn more. 0% on the next dollar earned at source; pay on spend.
Government role Auditor of annual income stories. Monitor of transactions and refunds.

Twenty percent at the till, partial relief in the chain

Retail faces a flat 20% GST in the model—one rate, fewer "is this a material or a service?" wars. PAYE disappears: your gross wage is your take-home until you choose to spend. That visibility is the political heart of the design; the fiscal heart is that businesses no longer reclaim all of their input GST by default.

The $100 B2B story (65% recovery)

On a typical business-to-business bill for $100 plus GST, the invoice is $120. Instead of getting the full $20 GST back, the buyer claims 65% ($13) and wears the remaining $7—about 7% of the underlying price. That "swallowed" slice is how the blueprint replaces a large slice of company tax without waiting for year-end profit. Stack that across many B2B hops and prices can snowball unless tiered relief is tight—see cascading and tiered recovery for the design story on raw materials and evidence gates.

  1. Pay: $120 leaves the purchaser's account.
  2. Claim: $13 refund (65% of $20 GST).
  3. Retain: $7 remains with the Crown as operational wedge.
  4. Tiers: narrow 100% / 85% lanes for absorbed or essential legs where statute allows—default stays 65%.

Red tape that disappears— and new vigilance

If the rails work, employers shed payday filing complexity; many households shed the annual return ritual; firms spend less life on depreciation schedules written for a profit base the state no longer chases in the same way. The trade is real: refund integrity, border parity, and anti-carousel work become central—spelled out under skeptic rebuttals in the risk note.

Closing the honest gaps

The blueprint leans on visible patches so the story is not "GST up, tough luck." Offshore retail, cross-border services, low-income households, and vertical integration each get a named answer in the full series; the pattern is: equalise borders, gross up transfers, police integration, reverse-charge offshore invoices.

Risk Mitigation (headline)
Offshore competition Border equalisation so foreign shelves pay a parallel wedge.
Low-income vulnerability ~15% welfare gross-up against higher shelf prices.
Vertical integration Commerce Commission mandate on tax-motivated in-sourcing.
Overseas services Reverse charge so NZ buyers self-assess GST under the same recovery rule.

Your pocket in two sketches

In the series' central planning case (~14% price lift within a 12–14% band), a household on about $140,000 gross keeps the full amount instead of roughly $109,000 after PAYE—enough, in the model's arithmetic, for a large discretionary gain after higher prices. A full-time worker near the minimum wage keeps roughly an extra $145/week from PAYE going to zero; if essentials rose with that central case, headroom is modest before gross-ups—why the transfer system is treated as co-equal with the headline wage story.
