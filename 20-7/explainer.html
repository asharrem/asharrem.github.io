Part 3. Plain-language orientation. Tables, revenue arithmetic, tier definitions, mitigations, and worked household numbers live in the executive summary; the conceptual guide develops the paradigm and extended examples. Here the focus is narrative: why the 1986 GST pivot left a hybrid "handbrake," what changes when tax follows velocity instead of declared success, and where to read the hard numbers next.

The 1986 Douglas reforms moved New Zealand from heavy reliance on income tax toward GST, but kept large direct bases. 20/7 is the proposal to complete the arc: no PAYE on wages, no company tax on profits, revenue from a uniform 20% GST plus a default partial refund on business inputs (65% recovery → a 7% wedge on the pre-tax price for most B2B legs), with tiered relief for absorbed materials and narrow essential chains (→ cascading note). Politically, the trade is obvious consumption tax at the till against full gross pay in your account; economically, the test is whether pass-through, compliance, and transfers hold inside the modeled band (→ risk framework).

What changes in plain words

  • Households: gross becomes take-home until you spend; shelf prices reflect higher GST and embedded business wedges; main benefits are indexed up in the blueprint so vulnerable lines are not an afterthought.
  • Firms: one statutory rate on invoices replaces a lot of category fights; the state's recurring take on operations is embedded in partial recovery rather than year-end profit debates.
  • Administration: the series argues IRD can pivot from forensic income work toward transaction rails—at the cost of new refund-integrity and border problems, not smaller government in every dimension.

For the B2B walk-through, fiscal table, mitigation list, and phased roadmap, continue from the summary into the guide and sectoral notes; for skeptic tests (cash economy, carousel refunds, OECD optics, sticky prices), jump ahead to risk: skeptic rebuttals once you have the baseline numbers in mind.
