The introduction of "Zero-Rated Raw Materials" is a double-edged sword. It solves the **Price Cascading** problem for essential goods like housing and food, but it risks re-complicating the system with the very "Accountant’s Logic" we are trying to escape.

To make this work within the **20/7 Model**, we would need a strict, objective definition of what qualifies as a "Zero-Rated Input" versus an "Operational Expense."

### 1. The Qualification Framework: The "Absorption Test"
In standard economics, we distinguish between **Intermediate Goods** (things that are transformed into the final product) and **Operating Expenses** (things that help the business run but aren't *in* the product). 

For the 20/7 policy, a raw material qualifies for a **0% invoice (100% refund position)** if it passes the **Absorption Test**:

| Category | Qualification Criteria | Example | 20/7 Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Direct Raw Materials** | Must be physically absorbed into or permanently part of the final sellable unit. | Timber in a table; Flour in bread; Concrete in a house. | **Zero-Rated (0%)** |
| **Indirect Inputs** | Essential for production but not part of the final product. | Electricity for the oven; Fuel for the truck; Welding gas. | **7% Surcharge** |
| **SaaS & Services** | Intellectual or digital tools used to manage or deliver the value. | Accounting software; Cloud hosting; Legal advice. | **7% Surcharge** |



---

### 2. The "SaaS" Debate: Is Software a Raw Material?
Your question about SaaS is the perfect example of the "Grey Area." A software developer might argue that **Amazon Web Services (AWS)** is their "raw material" because their app can't exist without it.

**The Policy Verdict:** In the 20/7 Model, **SaaS is NOT a raw material.** * **Why?** If we allow "digital inputs" to be zero-rated, every business in New Zealand will re-classify their entire office as a "digital factory." 
* **The Rule:** Only physical materials that undergo a **tangible transformation** qualify. Software, while vital, is an operational tool. By keeping SaaS at the 7% surcharge level, we maintain a clear, non-negotiable boundary that prevents the IRD from having to audit millions of "is this code a raw material?" claims.

---

### 3. Creating the "White List" (The Manageable List)
To avoid complexity, the government would publish a **Statutory White List** of raw material categories. If it’s not on the list, you pay the 20% and get the 13% refund.

**Proposed White List Categories:**
1.  **Primary Sector Extractive:** Raw timber, unprocessed ores, quarried stone, raw wool/pelts.
2.  **Bulk Agricultural:** Grains, seeds, raw milk, livestock (B2B only).
3.  **Industrial Feedstocks:** Raw steel, bulk resin/plastics, base chemicals.

> **The "B2B Only" Lock:** These items are only zero-rated when sold between GST-registered businesses. If a consumer buys a piece of timber at Bunnings, they pay the full 20%.

---

### 4. The Economic Outcome: "Clean Cascading"
By zero-rating these "Stage 1" materials, we solve the worst of the cascading tax.

* **Without Zero-Rating:** The 7% tax hits the tree, then the timber, then the frame, then the house. (Total cascade: ~21%).
* **With Zero-Rating:** The 7% tax *starts* at the timber-frame stage. (Total cascade: ~14%).

### Final Assessment: Is it worth the complexity?
**Yes.** As long as the definition is restricted to **Physical Absorption**, the list remains tiny and manageable. It protects the "First Mile" of production—our farmers, foresters, and miners—ensuring that New Zealand's base resources stay cheap, while the 7% surcharge still captures the "Value-Add" and "Operational" costs of the businesses further down the chain.
