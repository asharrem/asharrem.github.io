1. The paradigm shift: taxing 'creation' vs. taxing 'velocity'

The traditional model leans on vertical equity: higher shares of tax on higher income and profit. The 20/7 proposal instead leans on transactional breadth: the state clips flow and consumption while leaving marginal rates on labour and profit at zero in the blueprint. The 1986 reforms began the mixed GST-plus-income-tax world; this design is the argument for finishing the swing.

Feature The productivity brake (current) The velocity engine (20/7 model)
Primary target Production: Fines what people earn and what businesses profit. Process: Captures the flow of money through consumption and supply chains.
Psychological incentive Discourages marginal effort (overtime) and expansion due to higher tax brackets. Rewards effort; every extra dollar earned is kept entirely by the individual or firm.
Economic goal Revenue through direct "fines" on labor and success. Revenue through "velocity fees" on economic activity and spending.

The next sections spell out why partial recovery is a deliberate use of an existing rail—not a cosmetic GST tweak—and what that implies for households and institutions.

2. The mechanics of tiered recovery (65% default)

Today, firms usually reclaim 100% of GST on inputs. Under 20/7, most operational spend follows a 65% path: on a $100 taxable supply with $20 GST, the purchaser receives $13 back and retains $7 as a non-refundable operational cost—about 7% of the ex-GST price. That is the replacement wedge for much of what company tax raises today, collected continuously rather than after profit calculation.

Tiering (story version): physically absorbed inputs can earn 100% recovery on a narrow white list; a tight 85% lane can apply to selected essential-chain legs. Everything else stays on the default—otherwise the cascade story in cascading unravels slowly, then all at once.

Interpreting, not just tabulating: partial recovery makes every honest operator pay something on throughput even in loss years, which is exactly what makes the politics of abolishing CIT fiscally imaginable—and exactly what makes long chains politically sensitive.

At roughly $24.50/hour (40 hours), gross weekly pay is near $980; PAYE today removes about $145, so take-home is near $835. With PAYE at zero, the same gross lands in hand—then the price path bites on essentials. The weekly table in summary §2 matches the same story to the dollar; the policy point is the shape: dignity from keeping marginal hours, and transfers grossed up so the floor moves with shelves.

4. Social stability and strategic mitigations

The blueprint bundles patches so the velocity story is not regressive by neglect:

  1. Welfare gross-up (~15%): benefits and NZ Super rise with the modeled price path.
  2. Border equalisation: offshore consumer platforms face a parallel wedge so locals are not structurally undercut on tax alone.
  3. Reverse charge: NZ firms self-assess foreign services under the same recovery limits.
  4. Anti-cascade relief: strict absorption tests and narrow enhanced lanes—see cascading.
  5. Commerce Commission: expanded brief against tax-motivated vertical integration.

5. The death of bureaucracy: halving the IRD

The 20/7 model shifts the Inland Revenue Department (IRD) from a "forensic" system—investigating lives and profits—to a "transactional" system, where tax is captured as money moves.

Task Current bureaucratic headaches 20/7 streamlining
Individual filings Millions of returns, PAYE codes, and audits. Zero. Returns are abolished.
Business reporting Complex profit, FBT, and depreciation math. Zero. Tax is "clipped" off invoices.
Government focus Tracking "profit" (easily hidden/gamed). Tracking "spending" (hard to hide).

The economy becomes effectively self-policing. Because spending is far more difficult to hide than income or profit, the need for a massive, investigative bureaucracy is halved, allowing the state to pivot from auditing its citizens to simply monitoring the flow of the engine—within the limits of cash consumption and refund integrity spelled out in the risk framework.

6. Synthesis

The national $3.1 billion surplus story is still anchored to the revenue components in summary §1. At the level of ideas, 20/7 is a wager that a high-friction income base can be traded for a flow base if compliance, tiering, and transition inflation stay inside modeled bands—threads the sectoral, housing, risk, and verdict notes pick up next.
