2. Is it "Fair"? (The Equity Debate)
In tax policy, there are two types of fairness:
Horizontal Equity: People earning the same pay the same. (The 20/7 model excels here).
Vertical Equity: People who earn more should pay a higher percentage. (The 20/7 model struggles here).
The Re-imagination: A "fair" system usually requires a progressive element. The 20/7 model achieves "Prosperity" by letting high-earners keep their money to invest, but it achieves "Fairness" differently. It assumes that wealthy people spend more. By taxing the spending rather than the earning, the wealthy still pay more in total dollars to the state, even if the percentage is flat.
However, to be truly "Fair," the 20/7 model relies entirely on the Welfare Gross-Up. Without it, the system would be regressive. With it, you create a "Floor" that protects the poor while removing the "Ceiling" that limits the successful.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. Is it "Prosperous"? (The Growth Engine)
Most existing tax systems (like the US or UK) are "Leaky Buckets." They spend huge amounts of bureaucracy trying to define what is "Income" vs. "Capital Gains" vs. "Profit."
The 20/7 Advantage: By making income 0%, you plug all the leaks. There is no point in having a complex Trust, an offshore shell company, or a creative accountant because Income isn't taxed anyway. * This makes the 20/7 model the most "Prosperous" system imaginable. It turns the entire country into a Special Economic Zone.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. Is it "Sustainable"? (The Resilience Check)
The biggest risk to any tax system is Volatility. Income tax drops sharply during a recession when people lose jobs.
The 20/7 Resilience: Because the 20/7 model taxes spending and business operations, it is incredibly stable. Even in a recession, people still buy food, pay rent, and use electricity. Because businesses pay the 7% surcharge on their overheads rather than their profits, the government's revenue doesn't vanish just because a company had a bad year.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The Final Verdict: How does it stack up?
If I were to re-imagine a system from scratch, the 20/7 Model is the boldest "Growth" play available.
Compared to the Current System: It is Superior for Growth but Riskier for Inflation. It removes the "Productivity Brake" but adds "Supply Chain Friction."
Compared to the Nordic Model: It is Less Socially Cohesive (as it doesn't redistribute wealth as aggressively) but More Economically Dynamic.
The "Simple" Paradox: The reason it sounds "unbelievable" is that we have been conditioned for a century to believe that "Tax" must be a complex audit of a person's life. The 20/7 model proves that you can fund a state simply by "clipping the ticket" on the economy's movement.
The Verdict: The 20/7 model is the "Formula One" of tax systems. It is designed for maximum speed and efficiency. It is fairer to the hard-worker, more prosperous for the entrepreneur, and more sustainable for the state treasury. Its only "Disadvantage" is the political courage required to move away from the 100-year-old tradition of taxing income.
