The impact on a minimum wage earner is perhaps the most critical "Proof of Concept" for the 20/7 model. To ensure this policy is fair, we have to look at the interaction between the **removal of income tax** and the **Welfare/Minimum Wage Gross-Up.**

As of 2026, the New Zealand minimum wage is approximately **$24.50 per hour**. For a full-time worker (40 hours/week), the shift looks like this:

### 1. The Income Math (The "Weekly Winfall")

Under the current system, a minimum wage earner is taxed at 10.5% for the first $15,600 and 17.5% for the remainder. 

| Metric | Current System (Weekly) | The 20/7 Model (Weekly) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Gross Pay (40 hrs)** | $980.00 | $980.00 |
| **Income Tax (PAYE)** | ($145.00) | $0.00 |
| **Take-Home Pay** | **$835.00** | **$980.00** |
| **Net Weekly Increase** | - | **+$145.00** |

**The Result:** The worker receives an immediate **17% increase** in their weekly "cash-in-hand."

---

### 2. The Cost of Living Offset
Because shelf prices are expected to rise by **14%** (due to the 20% GST and the 7% B2B pass-through), we must calculate if that $145 extra covers the hike.

* **Current Weekly Spend:** If the worker spends their entire $835 on rent, food, and transport.
* **New Cost for Same Goods:** $835 + 14% = **$951.90**.
* **The Surplus:** $980 (New Pay) - $951.90 (New Cost) = **+$28.10 per week.**

**Verdict:** Even without a wage increase, the minimum wage earner is **better off by roughly $1,460 per year.**



---

### 3. The "Gross-Up" Protection
The 20/7 policy includes a mandatory **15% Gross-Up** for all government transfers (Working for Families, Accommodation Supplement, and Student Allowances).

* **Scenario:** A single parent on minimum wage also receives $200/week in Working for Families tax credits.
* **The Adjustment:** Under 20/7, that $200 is "Grossed Up" to **$230**.
* **The Impact:** This extra $30 is pure "inflation protection," ensuring that the 20% GST doesn't erode the support they rely on for essentials like housing and childcare.

---

### 4. Psychological & Economic Benefits for Low Earners
* **The "Overtime" Incentive:** Currently, if a minimum wage worker takes an extra shift, they often worry about "moving up a tax bracket" or losing their benefits (the "Abatement Trap"). Under 20/7, **every extra hour worked is 100% theirs.** If they work 5 hours of overtime at $36.75 (time-and-a-half), they keep all $183.75.
* **The Savings Ladder:** For the first time, a minimum wage earner can see a direct path to savings. Because the tax is only paid when they *spend*, they can choose to live frugally for a year and keep 100% of their earnings in a high-interest savings account (which also earns 0% tax on interest).



### 5. The Risk: Rent Inflation
The primary risk for minimum wage earners is **Rent**. While rent is typically GST-exempt in NZ, landlords will pay the 7% "Operational Surcharge" on their own costs (rates, insurance, maintenance). 
* **Mitigation:** The 20/7 policy assumes that the 15% Gross-Up of the **Accommodation Supplement** handles this specific pressure.

### Final Assessment
For the minimum wage earner, the 20/7 model is a **"Dignity Shift."** It moves them from being a "subsidized" citizen who pays tax only to get it back in credits, to a "productive" citizen who keeps every cent they earn and chooses how to contribute to the state through their consumption.
