1. Introduction: Deconstructing the "Productivity Brake"

New Zealand’s current architecture taxes the creation of value—wages and profits—through progressive and corporate schedules that are familiar but friction-heavy. The 20/7 proposal asks whether the state should instead anchor revenue in velocity: a high, uniform GST and a partial input-recovery rule that embeds tax in B2B flow, paired with explicit transfer gross-up so equity is not an afterthought.

Proponents argue the shift turns IRD from an "auditor of lives" into a processor of transactions, captures parts of the shadow economy at the till, and removes marginal tax on effort; critics worry about visible prices, cascade, and social licence. The executive summary holds the line-by-line revenue bridge; comparison explains why the full stack is sui generis internationally. This report walks the strategic arc—then ends with measurable gates, not cheerleading.

2. Historical Evolution: From the 1986 Douglas Reforms to the Pure Consumption Pivot

Legitimizing a radical fiscal pivot requires an understanding of historical trajectory. The 20/7 model is not a sudden aberration but the final realization of the "Rogernomics" journey that began with the 1986 Douglas Reforms.

The 1986 reforms were revolutionary for their time, introducing GST at 10% and slashing top marginal rates from 66% to 33%. However, those reforms resulted in a "hybrid system"—a compromise that maintained a significant handbrake on income while introducing consumption tax. The 20/7 model "finishes the journey" by stripping away the hybrid elements, removing the income tax component entirely, and evolving the GST from a simple value-added tax into a comprehensive operational levy.

By moving to a zero-income state, the 20/7 model eliminates the administrative friction and economic suppression inherent in the current hybrid architecture. This leads us to the specific mechanics that allow such a transition to remain fiscally neutral.

3. Structural mechanics: the 65% recovery rule

The design rejects split B2B versus retail statutory rates—too much "grey risk" litigation over whether a transaction is a material or a service— in favour of one 20% face rate on the invoice and differentiated refundability instead. Early shock models were discarded as too inflationary; the settled story is partial recovery on most operational inputs.

Walkthrough ($100 supply):

  1. Transaction: A business pays $120 for $100 of taxable supply plus $20 GST.
  2. Recovery: It claims 65% of the GST component—$13.
  3. Wedge: $7 remains as a non-refundable operational cost—about 7% of the ex-GST price.
  4. Tiers: narrow 100% / 85% lanes for absorbed or essential legs where statute and evidence allow—see cascading.

Repeated across many B2B stages, that wedge is the cascade problem; tiering and border policy are how the blueprint tries to keep the snowball from eating housing and food pipelines. The national yield arithmetic remains tabulated in summary §1.

4. The Export Paradox and Global Competitiveness

Critics of consumption-heavy systems often cite the "Export Paradox," suggesting that a 7% non-refundable surcharge on local inputs (power, rent, logistics) will handicap New Zealand exporters. This concern stems from the reality of "Tax Stacking," where the 7% surcharge compounds through various stages of the domestic supply chain (see Cascading, raw materials, and tiered GST recovery).

However, from a strategist's perspective, this stacking is a calculated trade-off. While local input costs rise, they are dwarfed by the windfall of a 0% Corporate Income Tax. For high-value, profitable producers—such as specialized tech firms or high-margin dairy exporters—the ability to reinvest 100% of their bottom-line profit is a massive "Supply-Side Catalyst."

This framework acts as an "Efficiency Filter." It intentionally "cleanses" the economy of low-margin, inefficient "zombie" industries that rely on tax deductions to survive. High-growth, high-value firms can easily absorb the operational surcharge in exchange for the total removal of profit tax, thereby aligning national fiscal policy with the goal of high-productivity growth.

This focus on efficiency, however, necessitates a fundamental re-imagining of social equity.

5. Equity Re-imagined: The 20/7 Model vs. the Nordic Benchmark

Redefining "fairness" is essential for the political viability of the 20/7 model. This system explicitly breaks the "Vertical Equity" rule—the traditional 20th-century notion that those who earn more should pay a higher percentage of their income. Instead, it prioritizes "Horizontal Equity," ensuring that the state captures a fair contribution based on an individual’s or firm’s economic footprint (spending) rather than their productivity (earning).

7. Systemic Risks and Strategic Mitigations

The 20/7 model is a high-reward framework that requires robust regulatory safeguards to prevent market distortions. Published-argument stress tests—informal economy, carousel and border imports, OECD Pillar Two, and sticky transition pricing—are gathered in the risk framework.

  1. Vertical Integration (The "Service Sector Killer"): There is a risk that large corporations will fire external specialist contractors (IT, cleaning, legal) and internalize those roles to avoid paying the 20% GST on invoices (where they lose 7% of the refund). To mitigate this, the Commerce Commission will receive an expanded mandate to monitor and penalize "Tax-Motivated Vertical Integration." Furthermore, the 7% rate is calibrated to be lower than the typical management overhead of internalizing staff.
  2. The Temu Loophole: To prevent offshore retail giants from gaining an unfair 7% advantage over local shops that pay domestic overheads, a 7% Border Equalization Levy will be applied to all consumer imports. This ensures every product sold in New Zealand contributes equally to the state.
  3. Overseas Service Invoicing: To prevent "transfer pricing" or tax dodging through offshore parent companies, a Reverse Charge Mechanism will be implemented. NZ businesses buying offshore services must self-assess the 20% GST and adhere to the 65% recovery limit, ensuring the 7% surcharge is captured locally.

Addressing these risks allows for a clear, phased implementation roadmap toward a zero-income state.

9. Strategic Decision Framework: Go / No-Go Gates

From a macro-strategy perspective, the central question is not whether the 20/7 architecture is intellectually coherent—it is—but whether it should replace world-norm mixed tax systems in one move. The recommended posture is phased adoption with hard empirical gates, not a single-step "big bang." This preserves upside while limiting transition risk.

The decision rule can be stated plainly: proceed to each implementation phase only if inflation, fiscal position, and social-license indicators stay inside pre-committed bands for a sustained monitoring window.

Gate Go threshold (proceed) No-Go trigger (pause / redesign)
Inflation calibration Observed transition inflation remains within the modeled 12–14% range, with no persistent overshoot beyond the 14% central planning case. Persistent overshoot above the band, or evidence that sector pass-through is broadening faster than modeled assumptions.
Fiscal neutrality Projected and realized revenue remain near neutral-to-surplus against baseline expenditure, with no structural gap opening after one-off effects. Material, repeated deficits emerge without credible offset from measured velocity growth or compliance gains.
Household cash-flow test Median and lower-decile households maintain positive net cash-flow after PAYE removal, modeled price effects, and transfer gross-up. Essential-spend pressure dominates wage/tax gains for vulnerable cohorts, or housing/rent pass-through outruns gross-up channels.
Sector stress test Low-margin, long-chain sectors remain operational with targeted tiering and productivity support; no systemic supply contraction. Widespread margin compression, exit waves, or persistent shortages in essential long chains (food, housing, core services).
Compliance integrity Refund controls, border treatment, and reverse-charge enforcement hold; fraud leakage remains contained within tolerance. Escalating refund abuse, informal-economy leakage, or border arbitrage materially degrades the modeled tax base.

Phase discipline: if two or more No-Go triggers fire concurrently, freeze progression to the next phase and activate corrective settings (narrower enhanced-recovery lanes, temporary anti-pass-through enforcement, or phased-rate hold periods) before resuming the roadmap.

This framework reframes 20/7 from a binary ideological leap into a measurable macro program: ambitious in direction, conservative in execution.

10. Conclusion: Sustainability and the "Formula One" Verdict

The strategic necessity of abandoning the 100-year-old tradition of taxing income is absolute. The 20/7 model represents the "Formula One" of fiscal systems: it is built for maximum speed, transparency, and economic velocity. By trading a high-friction, audit-heavy world for a low-friction, high-growth one, New Zealand effectively transforms itself into a Special Economic Zone.

Furthermore, this model is inherently more sustainable. Unlike income tax, which collapses during recessions when unemployment rises and profits vanish, a consumption-based system is resilient. Even in downturns, the velocity of money continues through essential spending and business operations, ensuring the state maintains its $3.1 billion surplus. The 20/7 Transformation is the ultimate supply-side play—a system that protects the vulnerable through targeted gross-ups while finally removing the handbrake on New Zealand’s national wealth.
