Part 9. Policy essay: the cultural and political read, not a duplicate of the executive summary. Numbers below are the same illustrative anchors the series uses elsewhere; the fiscal bridge table lives only in the summary.

For decades New Zealand has run a hybrid: meaningful GST and meaningful PAYE and company tax. Personal and corporate income taxes still raise on the order of $87.5 billion—money that arrives, in part, because the system fines success at the margin. The 20/7 pitch is not to nibble at rates but to change the default story: reward the extra shift at source, collect where money already moves, accept that receipts will look uglier while pay slips look cleaner.

The paycheck story people will actually feel

A dual-income household near $140,000 gross today might see roughly $109,000 after PAYE; under 20/7 they keep the full $140,000 until they spend. Against a modeled 12–14% price path (14% central case), the series still finds a large discretionary improvement—enough to change saving, debt, and renovation decisions if behaviour holds. At the minimum wage the directional story is different: PAYE relief is real, but essentials eat most of the lift unless transfers move with prices; that is why gross-ups are not a footnote. Full weekly table: summary §2.

Under the business hood

The corporate headline is 0% CIT; the replacement is a thin, repeated wedge on B2B flow via partial GST recovery—not a second corporate return, but a different compliance surface. High-margin firms with shallow domestic chains can treat the wedge as an entry fee to a world without profit tax; low-margin, long-chain operators live closer to the stress tests in sectoral and cascading. Exporters hear both truths at once: local inputs cost more, export profit is not taxed at source in the blueprint—what the series calls the export paradox.

Smaller IRD, different risk

The popular image is halving forensic audit culture in favour of transaction monitoring—fewer bracket puzzles, more refund integrity. That is energising as rhetoric and sobering as operations: cash still exists, carousel schemes still tempt, and offshore platforms still arbitrage attention spans. The risk framework collects the skeptical press on those failure modes.

Roadmap without magical thinking

The staged GST and income-tax step-down in the series is a narrative about buying time for software, transfers, and social licence—see summary §5 for the year-by-year sketch. The deeper question is whether politics can hold a multi-year transition without panicking at the first overshoot in a CPI print.

The evaluative report that follows names explicit go/no-go gates; comparison already argued why almost nobody runs this exact stack abroad.

Closing provocation: If gross pay became take-home overnight, would you spend differently on day one—or would you wait to see if prices stuck? How voters answer that in their heads is as important as any spreadsheet in the summary.
