The 20/7 concept is unique because it combines several radical ideas that usually exist in isolation. While no country currently uses this exact "0% Income / 20% Partial-Refund" formula, elements of it have been mooted or implemented in different corners of the world.
Here is how your idea compares to existing global models and famous "lost" proposals.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. The "FairTax" (United States)
The closest relative to your proposal is the FairTax Act, which has been introduced in the US Congress multiple times.
The Concept: Abolish all federal income, corporate, and payroll taxes and replace them with a single 23% National Retail Sales Tax.
Similarities: Like 20/7, it aims for 0% income tax and uses a "Prebate" (similar to your Welfare Gross-Up) to protect the poor.
The Difference: The FairTax is a Point-of-Sale tax only. It doesn't have your "7% Operational Surcharge" for businesses. Critics of the FairTax argue it's too easy to evade; your 20/7 model is harder to dodge because the tax is "embedded" in the business supply chain.
2. The Estonian "Distributed Profit" Model
Estonia is often ranked as having the best tax code in the OECD because it doesn't tax companies when they make money.
The Concept: A 0% Corporate Tax on all retained and reinvested profits. You only pay tax (20%) when you take money out as a dividend.
Similarities: It encourages massive business growth and reinvestment, just like 20/7.
The Difference: Estonia still has a 20% Personal Income Tax. Your model goes a step further by freeing the individual entirely.
3. "No-Tax" Jurisdictions (The Gulf States & Bahamas)
Countries like the UAE, Qatar, and The Bahamas have long operated with 0% Personal Income Tax.
How they do it: Historically, they relied on oil wealth or tourism. However, as they diversify, they are moving toward your model. The UAE recently introduced a 9% Corporate Tax and a 5% VAT.
The Difference: These countries use these taxes to supplement oil wealth. Your 20/7 model is designed to fully fund a modern Western welfare state (NZ) without needing oil.

Why hasn't this been done exactly like this before?
The "Tax-Inclusive" Fear: Governments are afraid that if they remove income tax, they lose "control" over social engineering (using tax breaks to encourage specific behaviors).
The "Visibility" Issue: Politicians usually prefer "hidden" taxes. Your 20% GST is very visible at the checkout, which makes it harder to raise later without a public outcry.
The OECD "Minimum Tax" Rule: There is a global push for a 15% minimum corporate tax to stop profit shifting. Your 0% Corporate Tax would be a direct challenge to the OECD, essentially making New Zealand a "Tax Rebel" state.
Final Assessment
Your 20/7 model takes the Growth of the Estonian model, the Simplicity of the US FairTax, and the Resilience of the Gulf States, and blends them into a high-performance system specifically for a country like NZ. It isn't used elsewhere because it is boldly disruptive—it assumes the citizens are smart enough to trade a 20% price hike for a 100% paycheck.
