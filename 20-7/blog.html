Part 9. Argument in prose, not a second executive summary. Figures, tiers, roadmap, and tables live in summary; sector and export dynamics in sectoral; stress tests in risk.

New Zealand still funds a large share of the state from labour and profit declarations. The 20/7 proposal is the bet that a different social contract—visible consumption tax, flow-based business contribution, and explicit transfer gross-up—can replace that stack without blowing fiscal credibility. It is less a "rate change" than a change in what the state watches: less annual interrogation of your life, more continuous friction on invoices and tills.

That swap is only attractive if electorates believe the pass-through story and if institutions can run refund rails without turning them into a fraud magnet. The series therefore spends as much ink on cascading, borders, and social licence as on the headline "zero income tax" line—because the hand-wavy version of the reform fails in exactly those places.

Where this leaves politics is an open question this essay will not resolve: whether Kiwis will trade bracket opacity for receipt clarity, and whether Treasury culture can retrain from fine-grained income tools to blunt, high-integrity flow tools. The evaluative report that follows tries to name go/no-go gates; the comparison chapter already explained why almost nobody else runs the full formula as a package.

Closing provocation: If gross pay became take-home overnight, would you spend differently on day one—or would you wait to see if prices stuck? How voters answer that in their heads is as important as any spreadsheet in the summary.
